Tax & Compliance

🏛 Tax & Compliance Notes (501(c)(8))
✅ Confirmed: No additional filings required for this investment structure

UBTI (Unrelated Business Taxable Income): Exchange-traded funds held by a fraternal benefit society under IRC §501(c)(8) do not generate UBTI as long as the fund itself does not use leverage or hold debt-financed property. All seven candidates here are plain-vanilla equity ETFs — no leverage, no derivatives, no debt financing. UBTI exposure: none.

Form 990: Investment income from qualifying ETFs is reported on Form 990 Schedule D and Part VIII. Dividend income of ~$518–$1,151/year (depending on the board's selection) falls within existing reporting lines. No new disclosure categories are triggered.

IRS §4941 (self-dealing): Not applicable. All funds are publicly traded, arm's-length instruments. No board member has a reportable interest in any of the issuing asset managers.

State of Vermont: Vermont does not impose a separate corporate tax on 501(c)(8) investment income. No Vermont-specific filing changes are required.

This summary is provided for informational purposes. The association's tax counsel should be consulted before any material change in investment policy.

Does NULV's partial petroleum exclusion satisfy the lodge's investment policy?

Glossary of Terms

Not familiar with investment terminology? This glossary explains every term used in this document in plain language — no finance background required.

ETF Exchange-Traded Fund
A basket of stocks (or bonds) that you buy and sell as a single share on a stock exchange — just like buying stock in one company, except you're instantly diversified across dozens or hundreds of companies. ETFs are cheap to own, easy to trade, and don't require a minimum investment beyond the price of one share. All seven funds in this analysis are ETFs.
AUM Assets Under Management
The total dollar value of all investor money pooled in a fund. A fund with $2 billion AUM holds $2 billion worth of stocks on behalf of its investors. AUM matters here for liquidity — a fund with only $7 million AUM (like ESMV) may not have enough daily trading activity to absorb a $35,000 purchase without moving the price against you.
Expense Ratio also called the "Fee"
The annual percentage of your investment that the fund company keeps as its management fee — deducted automatically from the fund's assets, not billed separately. A 0.06% expense ratio on $35,000 costs about $21 per year. A 0.48% ratio costs about $168 per year. Lower is better, all else equal.
Dividend
A cash payment that a company makes to its shareholders, typically every quarter, as a share of its profits. Dividend-paying stocks tend to be mature, profitable companies (utilities, consumer staples, financials). When an ETF holds many dividend-paying stocks, it passes those payments through to you as a distribution. This is the income component of the investment — the lodge would receive these payments directly into its Schwab account.
Yield SEC Yield & TTM Yield
Yield expresses the annual dividend income as a percentage of the fund's current price. A 3.29% yield on a $35,000 investment means roughly $1,151 in dividend income per year. Two common measures appear in this document:
  • SEC Yield — a standardized 30-day forward-looking estimate required by the SEC; useful for comparing funds apples-to-apples.
  • TTM Yield — "Trailing Twelve Months" — the actual dividends paid over the past year divided by the current price; reflects what investors actually received recently.
Beta
A measure of how much a fund's price moves relative to the overall stock market (the S&P 500, which has a beta of 1.0 by definition). Lower beta means less movement — more stability.
  • Beta 0.52 (USMV) — when the market drops 10%, this fund tends to drop only ~5%. Strong downside protection.
  • Beta 1.05 (ESGV) — moves slightly more than the market. Less protection in a downturn.
For a capital-preservation sleeve, lower beta is better. The column header in the table notes this.
2022 Return
How much the fund's total value changed during calendar year 2022 — the last major market downturn, when rising interest rates caused the S&P 500 to fall roughly −18%. Because capital preservation is the primary goal here, a fund's 2022 behavior is the single most important historical data point: it shows how much real-world protection it provided when the market fell hard. SCHD lost only −3.2% that year while the market lost −18%.
Drawdown
The peak-to-trough decline in a fund's value during a market downturn — the worst loss an investor would have experienced if they bought at the high point and held through the low. A "shallow drawdown" means the fund fell much less than the broader market. Minimizing drawdowns is the central goal of this low-volatility sleeve.
Low-Volatility Strategy
An investment approach that deliberately selects stocks that historically move less than average — utilities, consumer staples, healthcare — rather than high-growth but unpredictable technology stocks. Low-volatility funds aim to participate in most of a bull market's gains while losing significantly less during downturns. USMV uses a mathematical optimizer to build the least-volatile possible portfolio; SCHD achieves similar stability by selecting only high-quality dividend payers.
Values Screen also "ESG Screen" or "Exclusion Screen"
A rule built into a fund's index that prohibits holding stock in companies engaged in specified activities. A tobacco screen means the fund will not own shares of companies like Altria or Philip Morris. An alcohol screen excludes brewers, distillers, and wine producers. A petroleum screen excludes oil, gas, and coal companies. Screens reduce the universe of eligible stocks and may affect returns — this document quantifies that cost explicitly (~$633/year in forgone dividends).
ESG Environmental, Social, and Governance
A broad framework for evaluating companies on non-financial criteria: how they treat the environment, their employees and communities, and how they're governed. ESG funds typically combine exclusion screens with positive rankings (prefer companies with strong labor practices, low carbon emissions). Not all ESG funds are the same — their specific exclusions vary significantly, which is why this analysis examines each fund's rules in detail rather than assuming "ESG" means any particular set of screens.
Index Fund
A fund that passively tracks a pre-defined list of stocks (an "index") rather than having a portfolio manager actively pick investments. Index funds are cheaper than actively managed funds because they require less human decision-making. Every fund in this analysis is an index fund — SCHD tracks the Dow Jones U.S. Dividend 100 Index; NULV tracks the TIAA ESG USA Large-Cap Value Index; and so on.
Liquidity
How easily you can buy or sell a fund without the act of buying or selling itself moving the price against you. A fund with $91 billion in AUM and millions of shares traded daily (like SCHD) is extremely liquid — a $35,000 purchase is a rounding error. A fund with only $7 million in AUM (like ESMV) may have days with almost no trading, meaning a $35,000 purchase could represent a large fraction of the day's volume and push the price up before the order fills.
Capital Preservation
An investment goal that prioritizes not losing money over maximizing gains. A capital-preservation portfolio accepts lower returns in exchange for greater stability — it should decline less than the market in bad years, even if it also gains less in good years. This is the primary stated objective of the $35,000 equity sleeve described in this document.
RIC Regulated Investment Company
A legal structure under the Investment Company Act of 1940 that governs how mutual funds and ETFs are organized and taxed. All ETFs in this analysis are RICs. This matters for the lodge because RICs issue a standard Form 1099-DIV at year-end — the same form a bank sends for savings account interest — rather than a Schedule K-1, which would require more complex tax reporting.
UBTI Unrelated Business Taxable Income
Income earned by a tax-exempt organization (like the lodge) from activities unrelated to its exempt purpose, which can trigger a tax liability. Normally, investment income — dividends, interest, capital gains — is excluded from UBTI for 501(c)(8) fraternal societies under IRC §512(b). This means the lodge can hold any of these ETFs without triggering additional taxes. The one exception: income from debt-financed property (e.g., using margin borrowing at Schwab) would convert otherwise-excluded income into UBTI. The lodge should never use margin.
Basis Points bps
A unit equal to 0.01% — one one-hundredth of a percent. Used to describe small differences in fees or yields without ambiguity. "20 basis points" = 0.20%. The fee difference between SCHD (0.06%) and NULV (0.26%) is 20 basis points, which on $35,000 works out to about $70 per year.
Sector Diversification
Spreading investments across different industries — technology, healthcare, utilities, financials, consumer goods, etc. — so that a downturn in one industry doesn't devastate the whole portfolio. Most low-volatility ETFs actively manage their sector weights to avoid becoming too concentrated in any one area of the economy.
Market Capitalization "market cap" or "large-cap"
The total market value of a company's outstanding shares — share price multiplied by the number of shares. "Large-cap" companies (like Apple, Johnson & Johnson, Coca-Cola) are worth hundreds of billions of dollars and tend to be more stable than "small-cap" companies. NULV and SCHD both focus on large-cap stocks, which contributes to their lower volatility.
